# Claim Types and Evidence Standards

Every key claim in the research output falls into one of four types. Match the evidentiary standard to the type.

## Factual

A directly verifiable statement of fact: a number, a date, a specification, a definition, an official status.

- **Evidence required**: one authoritative primary source is sufficient
- **Examples**: "Go 1.22 added range-over-func", "The FDA approved X on 2024-03-15", "Company Y raised $50M Series B"
- **Source preference**: official docs, regulatory filings, spec sheets, authoritative databases

## Interpretive

An analysis, explanation, or characterization of why something is the way it is, what it means, or how it works in practice.

- **Evidence required**: 2+ credible sources, preferably from different perspectives
- **Examples**: "This architecture scales poorly under write-heavy loads", "The ruling signals a shift in regulatory posture"
- **Source preference**: expert analysis, in-depth articles, technical deep-dives, practitioner experience reports
- **Flag when**: only one source supports the interpretation — label as "one analyst's view" or similar

## Comparative

A judgment about relative merit, ranking, trade-offs, or suitability between options.

- **Evidence required**: 2+ independent sources; ideally first-hand benchmarks, case studies, or structured evaluations
- **Examples**: "Tool A outperforms Tool B for batch processing", "Framework X has better developer experience than Y"
- **Source preference**: benchmarks, head-to-head reviews, migration case studies, official feature matrices
- **Flag when**: evidence is asymmetric (much more data available for one option than another) — note this explicitly

## Predictive

A forward-looking statement about what will, may, or is likely to happen.

- **Evidence required**: multiple credible signals (trends, expert forecasts, announced plans); clearly separate prediction from established fact
- **Examples**: "Analysts expect the market to reach $X by 2027", "The project roadmap targets v2.0 for Q3"
- **Source preference**: analyst reports, official roadmaps, trend data, expert commentary
- **Flag always**: label as projection, forecast, or expectation — never present predictions as facts
- **Distinguish**: announced plans (factual — someone said it) vs. expected outcomes (predictive — it might happen)
